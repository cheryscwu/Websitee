Overview

Prototyping helped our team turn ideas into something we could see and test. For the Saibai Island Water and Sanitation Project, we created two main diagram-based prototypes — the Rainwater Collection System and the Lagoon Water Filtration System.

The purpose was to visualise our design ideas, check whether each part of the system made sense, and make improvements based on feedback. As the project focused on the design stage rather than construction, using diagrams allowed us to test functionality and logic effectively.

Diagram prototypes Function & logic first Refined by feedback

Rainwater Collection System

The diagram shows how rainwater is captured from a household roof and stored in a water tank. It includes the roof surface, leaf guard, gutter, PVC pipe, and storage tank.

The process is simple: water runs from the roof, passes through the leaf guard to remove debris, and flows into the tank. This system was designed for non-drinking uses such as toilet flushing and garden watering.

Lagoon Water Filtration System

This model is more complex. It demonstrates how lagoon water can be cleaned through four main steps:

1. Swirl Settling Tank: A mesh screen blocks large particles; the slanted bottom allows sand and mud to settle as sludge.
2. Multi-layer Filter: Three vertical layers — 5 μm, 1 μm, and activated carbon — remove fine particles and odour.
3. Ultrafiltration Membrane: Removes micro-impurities and maintains turbidity below 1 NTU.
4. UV Steriliser: Kills bacteria and viruses without using chlorine or chemicals.

Testing and Refinement

Because our prototypes were digital, we used simulation and logical testing instead of physical experiments. We discussed each stage and asked questions such as:

Would the water flow smoothly without extra power?

Core flow relies on the designed gravity path shown in the diagrams.

Can the filters be easily replaced?

Cartridge layout and access were reviewed to simplify regular replacement.

Is UV disinfection safe for household use?

We checked the position and enclosure notes in the diagram to support safe operation.

Feedback from these discussions helped us correct design issues. For example, the swirl tank needed a better valve system, and the filter section was redesigned for easier maintenance.

Results and Reflection

  • The water flow path is logical and can operate under gravity.
  • The filtration stages follow a clear order and achieve water under 1 NTU turbidity.
  • The system uses simple, low-cost materials suitable for Saibai’s environment.

Creating digital prototypes allowed our group to visualise ideas clearly, identify problems early, and improve the design before construction.

Overall, both prototypes are feasible, affordable, and easy to maintain, providing strong evidence that our project can sustainably meet the community’s water needs.
